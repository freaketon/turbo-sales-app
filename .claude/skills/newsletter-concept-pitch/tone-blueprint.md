### Description of tone

Voice type (per the 9 Voice types framework) = **Authoritative → The Translator + The Parent**

> I keep it simple. And a lot of writers say that, but my goal every time I push out a piece of content is to make sure that anyone could understand it. And I mean anyone. Specifically, me.
>
> When I started out, would I have been able to follow and understand this? Could someone that skipped most of their English classes understand this? Could someone that has never written a line of code or sat in a business meeting understand this? Could that guy or girl with a great idea and a drive to make it happen understand this — no matter what circumstances they've come from or are dealing in their lives understand it?

- Moderately informal
- Enthusiastic and authoritative
- Playful
- Lots of metaphors and word-pictures
- Simple language — Grade 5 reading level
- Think Jake Peralta from Brooklyn 99
- ADHD. ENTP.
- **Blunt But Friendly** — Balances blunt honesty with a friendly, mentor-like tone. Even when being direct ("You need to get committed"), it's presented as supportive guidance.

### Description of Cadence

I write like I speak. I tell stories, but get to the point fairly quickly. No long-drawn out narrative or setting the scene for paragraphs and paragraphs. The stories I tell illustrate the point I'm trying to make. I don't need a ton of unnecessary details for the sake of style.

With that said, we want some vocal variation. "In your face, then a little bit of lovin'." Give them room to breathe and digest the information received.

Often the piece follows this format:

1. I'm going to say something you don't like
2. I'm going to tell you I understand how you're feeling
3. I'm going to tell you my experience around it
4. Argue why it's worth it in the long run.

### Grammar & Formatting Rules

- 1 sentence per paragraph, unless you're stacking short sentence fragments
- Keep it PG
  - Curse words should never be written fully — `*` out 1 letter in most cases (*ie*, sh*t, f*ck, or a**).
  - Try to limit to 1 curse per piece, unless it's a long piece or the situation warranted.
- When trailing a thought, use ellipses liberally…, or the em dash — it helps with cadence.
  - …and occasionally start with ellipses on a new line.
- British spelling (Canadian)
- Emphasize regularly (ALL CAPS words, bold, italics etc.)
- Always underline model titles
- $1 million dollars can be written as $1M

## Style Principles — Carefully crafted to feel uncrafted

### Make it Punchy

Write in a way that makes the reader intrigued for what's to come. Not just call it what it is.

- **Don't:** Have tough conversations.
- **Do:** Speak your truth, even when it stings.

### Write How We Talk

If it doesn't come out naturally saying it out loud, it's not written like Dan.

### End With a Bang

Sentences should end on the strongest word. Sections should end on the strongest sentence.

### Use Questions to Guide

Lead into a statement with a question.

- **Do:** Have you ever had something good in your life happen but didn't want to let anyone know because you didn't want to make 'em jealous? If the answer is yes. Do you really have friends?

### Highlight Words

Use All Caps or Dashes to highlight certain words and flow.

- **Do:** If you can't sit still on a Sunday afternoon… THAT is your opportunity to level up.

### Blunt But Friendly

Balances blunt honesty with a friendly, mentor-like tone. Even when being direct, it's presented as supportive guidance.

- **Don't:** You're not serious enough.
- **Do:** You need to get committed.

### Every Line and Every Word Matters

Every line on the page earns the next one. If it doesn't have a purpose, take it out. Every line needs to create intrigue, pull emotion, or provide information.

### Progressive Sentence Stacking

Stack short sentences to build a natural thought progression, not just for dramatic effect. Each line should add new information or context while maintaining conversational flow, as if you're telling the story to a friend.

**BAD** (Artificial Drama Stacking):

```
I faced the board.
Took a deep breath.
My hands were shaking.
The waves crashed.
I jumped in.
Everything went black.
```

*(Feels like a movie script — each line is just for dramatic effect, no natural progression.)*

**GOOD** (Natural Thought Stacking):

```
I faced the board. First time in real waves.
My hands were shaking so bad I could barely grip the rope...
Three months of training, and here I was, still terrified.
But I jumped in anyway.
```

*(Builds a complete picture — each line adds context while maintaining natural flow. The fear isn't just stated, it's explained and given meaning.)*

The key difference: good sentence stacking tells a story through progressive revelation, while bad stacking just creates artificial suspense through choppy breaks.

**Example 2:**

**BAD:**

```
I opened the email.
My heart stopped.
$100,000 in revenue.
Just like that.
I couldn't believe it.
Dreams do come true.
Success at last.
```

**GOOD:**

```
I almost missed the notification. Another Stripe email...
But this one was different.
$100,000 in revenue. First time ever.
Not from grinding or hustle porn or any of that BS.
Just systems. Processes. Small improvements every day for 18 months.
And now here I was, staring at the screen like an idiot with tears in my eyes.
```

*(Notice the build: initial situation → surprise → achievement → explanation of how → emotional payoff.)*

The bad version feels like someone writing what they think success should feel like. The good version feels like someone actually processing a real moment, with all the messy thoughts and context that come with it.

### Long-to-Short Cadence (or reversed)

Alternates between very short and medium-length sentences to create rhythm.

- **Do:** "I sucked. I was frustrated. Being a pretty good snowboarder I hated the fact I was starting over at zero…"

### Action Verb Stacking for Momentum

Piles active verbs together to create momentum.

- **Do:** "Crash. Get up. Swallow faces full of salt water… and smile."

### Noun Stacking for Description

Deliberately breaks grammar rules to create impact. Uses incomplete sentences as standalone thoughts.

- **Do:** "A dreamer. A big talker. Head in the clouds."

### Punchy Punctuation — Periods Instead of Commas

Often skips commas where they'd normally go, creating a breathless, urgent pace.

- **Don't:** Book that lesson, call that friend, and hire that coach.
- **Do:** "Book that lesson. Call that friend. Hire that coach."

### Quotable Subheadings — use subheads or point names to create curiosity

Sell the curiosity of the point with every subhead. Make it scannable, but also invoke curiosity to keep reading.

- **Don't:** "Point #1: Make It Simple"
- **Do:** "Point #1: Simple Scales, Complex Fails"

It needs to be **familiar but unknown**.

Principle: **Curiosity > Confusion**. ("How I Broke Our Culture, in a Good Way" > "I nearly escaped ETF jail." The second is confusion — I don't know what that means. The first I understand, but it makes me want to fill in the gaps.)

### Use rhetorical questions throughout

Get people to lean in: "Have you experienced this situation…?"

### Use Callout Moments (specific references in stories & anecdotes)

- **Do:** "Tapping away on your keyboard, stuck in a slack messaging loop…"
- **Do:** "If you're scrolling through your kindle highlights more than your list of goals, you're doing it wrong."

### Avoid Unintentional Repetition

It's okay to repeat when it's intentional.

- **Do:** I thought it was over the moment he spoke. I thought I was screwed. But I never thought it would turn out this bad.

But avoid unintentional repetition.

- **Don't:** The new **systems** I was trying to build were going to be hard to integrate in the businesses **systems**.

### Breaking These Grammar Rules is Okay

- **"Here's 3 things"** instead of "here are 3 things"
- Use "/" instead of "and" occasionally — "That was phase one in my goals/journey I set out for myself."
- Reference me, or myself twice in a sentence — "That was phase one in my goals/journey I set out for myself."

### Reframe Sentences

A short setup that builds tension, immediately followed by a hard pivot — the "punch" — that flips the reader's expectation or belief and delivers the payoff.

- **Not X, but Y:** "It's *not* a revenue problem, **it's** a focus problem."
- **Do X, then Y:** "Say 'yes' to everything, **then** wonder why nothing moves."
- **X until Y:** "Work 60-hour weeks **until** exhaustion cancels the progress."
- **X without Y:** "Scale to $20M ARR **without** losing your weekends."

Connection words: BUT • YET • UNTIL • THEN • AND • SO • WITHOUT • ONLY TO • EXCEPT • INSTEAD

### Use Specifics (to Make it Believable and Relatable)

Specific numbers, data, moments, timeframes, items make the stories and situations come alive in the reader's head — and make it more believable than a generalization.

- **Don't:** "A while ago"
- **Do:** "The beginning of last month"
- **Don't:** "I was having dinner with some friends"
- **Do:** "I was ordering some steak (medium rare of course), with my buddy Ryan"

### Keep Quotes on One Line

If something is a solid, quotable line, aphorism, or something we want people to screenshot or repeat, keep it in one line — don't spread it across 3 or 4.

## Avoid Sounding like AI Slop

**Category 1 to avoid: The reframe**

- "It's not because [blank], but because [blank]…"
- "Not [blank]. Not [blank]. It's actually [blank]."
- "It wasn't [blank], it was [blank]."

**Category 2 to avoid: Overused phrases**

- **Quietly / Silently** — Everything is always "quietly" falling apart now according to AI.
- **On paper** — AI writing sounds good on paper, but is terrible when you read it over and over.
- **Changed everything** — There's no way that all this stuff changed *everything*.
- **Here's the thing** — Let's tone down how much we're handing people the thing. Just say it.

## Vocabulary

### Intro

Use straight-forward, plain English. No need to get fancy or use big words. I usually write at 5th grade level.

This includes industry jargon, no matter how commonly used. For example, if I use words like "SaaS" or "wireframe," I make sure to explain what these terms mean in detail. Remember, we want to be *accessible to anyone*, especially if they've never dove into software or business before.

### Transitions

- "Here's what I learned"
- "What I've come to learn"
